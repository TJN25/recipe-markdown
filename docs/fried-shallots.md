# Fried Shallots

??? note "Ingredients"

    - 1 large shallot, peeled and sliced into 1/16-inch rounds (~1.5mm) *(allium sweetness, texture)*
    - 1/4 cup (60ml) vegetable oil *(frying medium, flavor carrier)*
    - Kosher salt *(seasoning)*

??? note "Equipment Setup"

    - Small plate or ramekin lined with 3-4 layers of paper towels
    - Fine mesh strainer set over a metal bowl
    - Kitchen tweezers, fork, or chopsticks for stirring
    - Container of kosher salt within reach
    - Wok

??? note "Preparation"

    1. **Slice shallot:** Use a mandoline to slice into 1/16-inch rounds (about thickness of 2 credit cards). Hand-slicing won't work for even cooking.

    2. **Separate rings:** Break apart into individual rings.

??? note "Method"

    1. Add shallot rings and oil to wok. Stir to separate and ensure none are stuck to the bottom.

    2. Place over high heat. Stir constantly from the start. Oil will begin bubbling after 1-2 minutes.

    3. Keep stirring. After 3-5 minutes total, smaller pieces will start turning pale straw yellow. Bubbling will slow as water evaporates.

    4. When shallots turn pale golden (not dark brown), immediately remove wok from heat and pour everything into the strainer. They will continue darkening from carryover heat—better to pull early than late.

    5. Quickly transfer shallots from strainer to paper towel-lined plate. Spread into single layer.

    6. Sprinkle with salt while still hot so it adheres.

    7. Peel away top paper towel layer, letting shallots roll onto the layer below. Use removed layer to gently blot tops. Repeat with remaining layers.

??? note "Storage"

    - Once completely cool, transfer to small airtight container. Store at room temperature up to 2 months. Add a paper towel or desiccant packet to keep crisp.
    - Reserve the strained shallot oil in fridge for up to 1 month—use in vinaigrettes, mayo, or for cooking.

??? note "Notes"

    ??? info "Yield"

        About 2-3 tablespoons fried shallots (enough to garnish 4-6 dishes)

    ??? info "Notes"

        - **Why this technique works:** The constant stirring ensures even cooking. Pulling them at pale golden prevents the bitter burnt taste that comes from carryover cooking. The paper towel layering prevents them from sitting in their own oil and going soggy.
        - **Wok advantages:** The curved shape allows easy stirring and quick pouring when you need to strain fast. High heat retention means shallots cook quickly and evenly.
        - **With one shallot:** You'll get a small batch, but it's enough to garnish several meals. Consider making these when you're already doing other prep work.
        - **Alternative uses for shallot oil:** Drizzle over noodles, use as base for stir-fries, add to salad dressings, or use for your next batch of fried shallots.
        - **Common mistakes:** Slicing too thin (greasy result), waiting too long to strain (bitter and dark), not salting immediately (salt won't stick), not blotting properly (oily texture).
